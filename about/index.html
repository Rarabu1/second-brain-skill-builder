The technology is there to serve you.

Not the other way around. AI is a force multiplier, not a replacement for human judgment. Whether that means doing more of the work that matters, automating what doesn't, or reclaiming time for things that have nothing to do with work: that's your call.

How this started

I work in the adversarial security space at a large tech company. We deal with the bad side of technology: how it can be misused, broken, or exploited. A few months ago, the organization decided that AI integration wasn't optional. That applied to everyone: engineers, operations, policy, communications. If you worked there, you were expected to figure out how to use it. The role didn't matter.

I figured it out. Then I went deeper. I built a workflow that handled things I used to do manually: drafting in my voice, remembering context across projects, surfacing what I needed before meetings, clearing the cognitive overhead that used to eat my mornings. It became something I relied on the way you rely on a good colleague.

What I noticed

Two things became clear when I watched others try to do the same.

First: this had taken me real time. I was genuinely captivated by the technology. I had the patience and the inclination to iterate. Most people I worked with didn't, and the gap wasn't ability. It was the on-ramp. A blank terminal screen reads as "this is for engineers." The question "will I break something?" is real, even when the answer is no. There were basic skills that needed to exist before the interesting work could start.

There's also a legitimate question underneath all of it: is this actually worth my time? Spending an hour setting up something that currently takes five minutes is a real trade-off. Sometimes the math works. Sometimes it doesn't. This site is built to help you find out faster, and to make the setup light enough that the answer is usually yes.

Second: the people who cleared that initial friction saw the same thing I did. Not a cheap chatbot. Not autocomplete. Something that actually changed how much they could do.

What happened next

I started sharing the process. Seminars inside the company. One-on-ones with colleagues trying to get past the setup. The same questions kept coming up: how do you actually build this, what files do you need, how do you make it remember who you are and how you work.

I realized the process could be useful outside the company too. This site is what I put together.

Make it your own

This is my process. It works for me.

Concretely: I write talking points for leadership and have them ready for review in minutes. I review and annotate policy documents. I've automated how I handle certain emails and chats. I triage my communications, inbox, and projects at the start of each day.

That might be directly useful to you. Or you might find slightly different applications depending on where you work and what you do. My goal isn't to give you a system to copy. It's to give you enough of the basics that you can build your own. Once you know a few chords, you can jam your own AI jazz album session.
