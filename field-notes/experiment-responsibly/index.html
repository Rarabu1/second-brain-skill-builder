Field Note 01 · Experiment responsibly

Go break something. Carefully.

I think about how technology gets misused, broken, and exploited for a living. That is exactly why I want you to go play with this. There is one line to draw before you start, and it is the whole of this first note: not at work. Not yet.

The one line to draw first

Before any of this goes near work, ask.

If you work at a large company, stop before you install anything on a work machine or point any of this at work data. Talk to your security team first. If your company has a CISO, this is their question to answer, and they would much rather you asked.

This is not about getting in trouble, although that is real. The better reason is that they may have already done the hard part for you. Plenty of enterprises have an approved version, an agreement that changes what happens to your data, a sanctioned path to the exact thing you are trying to do. You find that out by asking, not by guessing.

The move

Keep work out of it until work says otherwise. Walk in with "I want to use this, what is our approved way," and let them hand you the safe version. That is a good look. Getting discovered after the fact is not.

Almost nothing here is a showstopper

The answer is almost never a flat no.

Every industry carries its own rules. Regulated data, privileged material, records you are required to keep, information you are bound to protect. Here is the part people miss: all of it can be accommodated. Enterprises run these tools inside heavy regulation every day. The constraint is rarely whether you can, it is how, and the how depends on your industry, your data, and the specific tool.

So the answer is contextual, not forbidden. That is exactly why you ask first, instead of assuming it is off-limits and missing out, or assuming it is fine and finding out otherwise.

So where do you actually play?

Somewhere that is yours, on things that do not matter yet.

That is the rest of this series. Over the coming weeks I will take it apart one piece at a time: how to set up a place where a mistake is free, what is really happening when the tool asks for permission, what to watch for when it reads something you did not write, and what to check before you hand it anything sensitive.

None of it is exotic, and none of it is a reason not to start today. It is the difference between a tool you control and a tool that quietly controls you.

Drawing these lines for a team?

If you are a team or an organization trying to figure out where these lines are, that part is literally my job. Tell me what you are trying to do and what you are bound to protect, and I will help you find the version that is both genuinely useful and safe.
